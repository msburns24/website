Diagrams Advice






Three would add the most value, in rough priority order:

   1. Label distribution shift (Section 8 — Drift Results)

      This is the strongest case. The numbers are dramatic (34% positive → 13%,
      61% negative → 83%) but a side-by-side grouped bar chart makes the shift
      immediately visceral in a way a formatted text block doesn't. It's also
      the most "shareable" image in the post — the kind of thing that gets
      pulled out of context.

   2. The fallback chain (Section 5)

      The ASCII art works, but a proper flowchart with styled boxes —
      color-coded by tier (green/orange/red matching the tiers) and with the
      pass/fail routing labeled on the arrows — would be significantly more
      readable, especially on mobile. The presentation version with the three
      color-coded tier boxes is a better model than the monochrome ASCII. This
      is the diagram most likely to be referenced by readers who want to
      replicate the pattern.

   3. Confidence histogram — healthy vs. drifting (Section 6)

      The text in that section says "a healthy system has most mass in the
      0.8–1.0 bucket" and "a drifting system shows mass spreading toward
      0.4–0.6." That's a natural paired diagram: two small histograms side by
      side, one labeled "healthy" with a right-skewed distribution, one
      labeled "drifting" with a flatter or bimodal one. It's a diagram that
      teaches the concept, not just illustrates data you already have.


The system architecture diagram (Section 4) and the text-length KS comparison
are lower priority — the architecture ASCII is already legible and the KS
result is better conveyed by the p-value than by a density plot. I'd skip those
unless you're going long-form.

For format: Mermaid works well for the flowchart if your blog platform renders
it. The histogram and bar chart are better as static images since they show
actual data rather than structure.
